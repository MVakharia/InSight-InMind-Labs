## Context

I'm a former Team Fortress 2 player, and I've been playing Overwatch since 2018. One of the first things I noticed about Overwatch was that unlike TF2, the server browser was hidden away and not a part of the core game loop: the front-facing modes have always been Unranked (formerly Quick Play) and Competitive. This has always bothered me, and since I started studying the human mind, I've begun to see the wider implications of this design choice. 

This is where I'll talk about it.

### Introduction

Here I'll examine the industry-wide shift from prioritising persistent servers, available through a server browser, to prioritising ephemeral, session-based servers, available through matchmaking, and what we lost during that shift.

There are different types of game lobbies: Unranked, Ranked, rotating/casual/limited-time game modes, custom games, and community servers. 

The premise is that depending on the lobby, the following may change:

- How players learn the game
- How players feel they are ‘allowed’ to play the game
- The sense of community and identity that players feel within a game
- How players approach and engage in play, as well as how they behave, in any given server

I'll explore how the game environment reshapes the above as well as the emotional ecology of play: that is, the intersection of feelings and human emotions, social cues, role expectations, and feedback loops that emerge between players, game mechanics, and the community culture. 

I'll explore the behaviours that a multiplayer environment enables, supports, or suppresses through its structural design.

The primary examples that will be used are Overwatch and Team Fortress 2, but other examples will also be used.

I'll look at the "It’s just Quick Play, don’t take it so seriously" phenomenon and why it happens.

How does the culture around each game shape its emotional ecology?
