**What happens when a maintenance company values site visits over actual repairs?**

This is a story about a broken system. 

It's been a few years since I was in this role. I only lasted a couple of weeks before I decided I'd had enough and found another contract to get on board with, but this experience stayed with me.

It was a role where I'd spend 11 hours a day (10hrs + 1hr break) in the field, 5 days a week, so 55 hours per week, for £20 above minimum wage. By today’s standards, it’d be below minimum wage. 

This 55-hour contract was a new one for engineers, replacing the old ‘legacy’ contract of 40 hours per week, but for identical pay. Engineers on the 40 hour contract would continue working 40 hours weekly. 

I was tasked with servicing retail IT equipment in supermarkets, convenience stores, restaurants, and coffee shops. I'd do this in three counties that were 200-400 miles away from my home, which meant that half of my first and last days of the week would be eaten up by commuting. 

A hotel was provided in the nearest county, as well as morning and evening meals.

Within 10 hours, I was expected to hit 7 sites daily. 

The training consisted of a brief visit to an equipment room to have a quick look at all of the computer systems we'd be tasked with servicing. We probably spent no more than 30-60 minutes looking at all of the systems. We’d use this ‘knowledge’ to service the IT equipment of at least a dozen clients. 

I got to my first site and that's where the problems began: despite my ‘training’ and the IT experience I had developed both independently and as an engineer, I found myself lost, unable to repair the machine I was assigned. 

Over the next couple of weeks, I remember only closing a single ticket successfully. I dealt with a bully of a manager, I spent far too long at sites trying to get a real handle on the job, trying my best to learn how to fix these machines, and I neglected my health for the sake of getting the job done, and would usually be unable to hit all 7 sites per day because I was trying so hard.

It genuinely wasn’t clear if real work was even possible. 

But after I left the role, I realised something: not once had I ever got in trouble for failing to fix something. I did, however, get in trouble for not visiting every store in a single day. 

The entire situation was insane, but only later did I realise it was working exactly as intended, as long as you got with the program: doing very little actual work. 

When a system is that misaligned, you end up feeling like *you* failed, but in reality, you were never meant to succeed in doing the job as advertised on paper, because the workload is mathematically impossible in the time given. 

It’s a pattern that comes from dysfunctional field service setups. 

A realistic target for a break-fix engineer is 1-4 sites in an 8 hour day, depending on the complexity of the work. If it’s a 10 hour day, you push that to 5 sites, but only if it's all quick fixes that you're well-versed in.

7 sites a day in 10 hours isn’t a real operational expectation. It’s something that someone wrote on a spreadsheet to impress someone else. 

If you can arrive on site, read documentation for 30-45 minutes to try (and fail) and understand the problem, log the ticket as ‘site attended, documentation read, further work required’, pack up your things and move on, and still meet the target, it proves that the job isn’t about meeting outcomes; it’s about **motion**.

This is a classic symptom of a KPI-driven company culture with no grounding in reality. 

The managers at this company care only about reporting numbers upward, not about fixes. 

The quantity of sites visited is valued over the number of problems solved. 

The training is theatre: a performance to absolve the managers of any blame and put all of it onto you. They can just say they trained you even if they didn’t provide you with any meaningful information. 

The workforce is expected to absorb an impossible amount of information and meet equally impossible expectations. 

Your actual skill and your output is mostly irrelevant; your presence is all that matters. 

When a company sends untrained staff to fix machines, they’re really just using bodies to fill the gaps. 

As an aside, they’re hoping workers will self-teach under pressure. 

Coverage is prioritised over competence. Workers are bodies used to hit coverage metrics, not technicians. Coverage satisfies the client contract. 

The role of the manager in this scenario isn’t to take care of their workers, it’s to keep workers from questioning impossible targets. It’s to redirect blame down the ladder. It’s to maintain the illusion that everything is fine. And it’s to punish anyone that exposes that everything is actually on fire. 

A bullying manager keeps the broken system running. 

So what is an engineer actually supposed to do, in spite of the job description?

Show up, move between sites, absorb blame, keep the numbers looking good, never challenge the system, and never expose the impossibility of the targets. The system is built to absorb all of this. 

Actual fixes are optional. 

A worker who quietly travels between sites, repairs almost nothing, and doesn’t cause trouble is more useful than a worker who actually completes more repairs on average, but vocally challenges the system and fails to hit every site.

## Notes

In a company where problem solving is valued, the typical first-time-fix target would be 80-90% for beginner engineers, with a company average above 95%. You'd receive 40 hours of training on 1-3 machines that are all similar in some way, followed by several weeks of shadowing a senior engineer; you'd tag along on dozens of callouts before getting your first repair job.
